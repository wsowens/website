Research

Interests

My major interest is in molecular biology and molecular genetics. Specifically, I am very interested in the potential applications of these fields. By understanding biology on its most fundamental level, we can manipulate life itself.
After my first summer of research (see below), I realized just how important bioinformatics has become for biology research. I began studying computer science in my first semester at UF, and acquired an interest in that field as well. Especially fascinating to me are its overlaps with biology, namely its relation with artificial life.
Nevertheless, my primary passion is still biology.

Summer 2017 (April-August)

Returning to Bass Laboratories as a Research Assistant, I again worked under the direction of Dr. Hank Bass. I spent much of the first month working on the Maize-10-Maze-a large outreach event aimed at engaging the public with the world of maize genetics. At the FSU Research Fields, Bass Laboratories created a model of the maize genome, with mutants placed in the maze at positions corresponding to the genes that give rise to their phenotype. By bringing the maize genome to life, the maize-10-maze allowed the general public to literally step through the genome.

As the maize-10-maze was finishing, I shifted my focus towards actual research. In the laboratory, I helped with the in silico search for G-quadruplexes, indentifying candidate G4 sequences in both the human and maize genomes. In the process, I tested and implemented a yet-to-be published program developed by a veteran of the software industry. Ultimately, I left the lab with a library of scripts for the manipulation of FASTA and BED files, facilitating the processing of large volumes of genomic data.

Summer 2016 (May-August)

At my first Summer at Bass Laboratories, I worked as a volunteer Research Intern. I helped with undergraduate Savannah Savadel's efforts to track Nucleotide Diphosphate Kinase activity in Zea mays using epifluorescence microscopy. NDPK activity plays an important role in cellular metabolism, and has been shown to interact directly with G-quadruplexes.

Issues (primarily autofluorescence from the maize tissues) hampered the NDPK localization experiments. Autofluorescence, fluorescence from natural, cellular sources, introduces a great amount of noise to microscopy efforts. I assessed UV photobleaching as a means of reducing autofluorescence. I noted a significant reduction in UV autofluorescence after samples were exposed to several hours of UV radiation, though the effects of UV radiation on the signal itself remains unchecked.
